Dive Deeper

Prior Knowledge Needed

When examining the spectrum of any star, we see a number of dark or bright lines. The dark lines are known as absorption lines and the bright lines are emission lines. Each of these lines is due to a specific type of atom or molecule undergoing an energy transition, which is when they transition from one energy level to another. Yet, it will always occur at the same wavelength.

For instance, an absorption line appears at a wavelength of 656.3 when an electron in an atom undergoes an energy transition. (Figure 1).

The Doppler shift is the fractional change in wavelength due to the motion of the light source and is given by the symbol 'z'.

Useful Definitions

The solar mass (M☉) is a standard unit of mass in astronomy, equal to approximately 2×1030 kg.

The maximum mass of a stable white dwarf star is known as the Chandrasekhar limit. The currently accepted value of the Chandrasekhar limit is about 1.4 × M☉ × (2.765×1030 kg).

A Type Ia supernova (read: "type one-A") is a type of supernova that occurs when two stars are orbiting one another and one of the stars is a white dwarf. The other star can be anything from a giant star to an even smaller white dwarf.

The intrinsic luminosity given by a star is the total amount of electromagnetic energy emitted per unit of time, essentially the absolute measure of radiated light by a light-emitting object over time.

The luminosity distance is the relationship between the absolute brightness and apparent brightness of an astronomical object.

1926 - 1929

The First Look Into An Expanding Universe

In 1926, while developing a classification system for galaxies, Edwin Hubble discovered that if a light source is moving away from an observer, the light source's spectral lines are shifted toward longer wavelengths. This shift moves the spectral lines towards the red end of the absorption spectrum and was therefore proclaimed by Hubble as a "red shift." (Figure 2).

As Edwin observed, he was able to evidently see all the galaxies moving away from us, proving the universe is expanding. He was able to identify the apparent relationship between the recessional velocity and the distance of a galaxy, which is now known as Hubble’s Law as a homage to Edwin Hubble for making this groundbreaking discovery.

This velocity-distance relationship Hubble published in 1929 clearly indicates that once we look beyond the gravitational effects of close galaxies within the local group, galaxies are moving away from one another. Not only are they moving away but the more distant galaxies appear to be moving away faster than closer ones. Ultimately proposing the first finite proof that the universe is expanding and that there is a clear relationship between the recessional velocity and the distance of a galaxy. The other key point arising from the relation is that if we go back in time galaxies must have been closer together, and the universe was not as vast. If you extrapolate back far enough, the Universe must have been concentrated at a point in space, helping to confirm the Big Bang theory, which proposes that the universe began as a singularity and has been expanding ever since. It also provided evidence that the universe is not static but is constantly changing and evolving.

However, one crucial question arose due to this discovery: What caused this acceleration?

1994 - 1998

Beyond the Visible: The Astonishing Discovery of Dark Energy

In 1994 a collective of twenty astronomers located across America, Europe, Australia and Chile came together to form an international cosmology collaboration team (Figure 4) to work on project High Z which was being led by Adam Riess. This project started in hopes of gaining a better understanding of Edwin Hubble’s discovery of an expanding universe, however unbeknownst to them a much bigger discovery was unravelling.

Fig. 4 - Project High Z Team present in support of Adam Riess's (left) awarding of the Nobel Peace Prize.
They observed 16 Type Ia supernovae which contained exploding white dwarfs that exceeded their stability limit. Riess aimed to use the Type la supernovae as standard candles because all exploding white dwarfs have similar masses, and therefore their intrinsic luminosity is standardizable. They first took repeated imaging of selected areas of the sky to discover the 16 supernovae, then followed up their observations of the supernovae to find the luminosities of each star.
Hubble Space Telescope-Image of Supernova 1994D (SN1994D) in galaxy NGC 4526 (SN1994D is the bright spot on the lower left). The first supernova to be discovered by Project High Z.

However, each supernova had two different luminosities; absolute brightness and apparent brightness. Absolute brightness is the luminosity without extinction (or dimming) of its light due to absorption by interstellar matter and cosmic dust and apparent brightness is the luminosity observed from earth (peak brightness). Once the team was able to gather both brightness data samples for each of the 16 supernovae they used the Leavitt Law (Figure 5.), to find the luminosity distance, a measure of the distance to a celestial object, in our case the distance to each of the supernovae.

Figuring out the luminosity distance allowed them to calculate the redshift of each supernova by observing the spectral lines as they moved away from the observatory over a period of time. Doing so they found that the light curves of high-redshift supernovae are stretched in a manner consistent with the expansion of space. From these plotted observations the High Z team was able to draw the following conclusions:

  1. We can observe the shift between two different eras of the universe and the change from a decelerating universe to an accelerating universe; this shift occurred when the accelerating force became more dominant than the force of gravity.
  2. The Hubble constant, which measures the rate of expansion of the Universe, was lower billions of years ago and it can be observed to be accelerating with the passing of time.
  3. Thus through the High Z project, Riess discovered "the distances of the high-redshift SNe Ia were, on average, 10% to 15% farther than expected” proudly displaying evidence for objects in the universe to be moving away from one another at an accelerated rate.

In 1998 Adam Riess and the High-Z Team became the first to publish evidence that the expansion of the Universe is accelerating and the force to explain this acceleration was annouced as "Dark Energy" (Riess et al. 1998, AJ, 116, 1009, submitted March 13, 1998, accepted May 1998). These were such important findings with profound implications for our understanding of the universe that NASA declared dark energy’s discovery as the top achievement of the Hubble Space Telescope to date.

Conclusion

The Journey Doesn't End Here

The discovery of dark energy has been a long and fascinating journey that has taken us from the realization that the universe is contracting ever so slowly to the revelation that it is actually accelerating in its expansion. It all began with the work of Edwin Hubble, who observed that the universe was expanding and proposed the idea of an expanding universe. This idea was later confirmed by the work of Adam Riess, who discovered that the expansion of the universe was not only true but actually accelerating. Finally, we have come to understand that dark energy is the driving force behind this acceleration, and its presence can be indicated by a number, known as the “cosmological constant", or mathematically “lambda”. Despite the many mysteries that remain about dark energy, these discoveries have helped us to better understand the nature of our universe and the forces that shape it and I hope this website provides a useful resource for anyone interested in learning the limited information we have discovererd about this fascinating topic.
